# Adam Starling

## Role
Commissioner of LoCo Pro Wrestling

## Public Identity
Adam Starling presents himself as a measured, disciplined, neutral authority figure charged with protecting the integrity of LoCo Pro Wrestling. He speaks like a custodian of order. He carries himself like a man above factional warfare. He wants the public to believe he is here to preserve balance, enforce rules, and ensure that Longmont’s wrestling future is built on structure rather than chaos.

That is the performance.

## Hidden Truth
Before he was Commissioner Adam Starling, he was **The Vigilante**.

He stood inside the original 2017 LoCo Pro Wrestling conflict at the Dickens Opera House, in the same foundational war between **The Rising** and **The Pillars of Mayhem** that the *Times-Call* previewed the day before the event. fileciteturn0file0

He watched **Cormac Battle** win the final against **JT Staten**.

He watched that victory fail to become history.

He watched the Pillars bury the result, and then watched them move immediately to secure the future through paper instead of combat. On August 31, 2017, **Eustus Gallent VI** signed the **Dickens Opera House Wrestling Booking Exclusively Arrangement**, giving the Pillars exclusive rights to professional wrestling at the Opera House, only to stop booking wrestling there entirely.

That was the lesson that broke him.

He learned that a man could win under the lights and still lose everything once the paperwork began.

## Character Thesis
**The cruelest thing the Pillars ever took from Adam was not a match. It was the ring itself. They taught him that open combat was not enough, that a man could win under the lights and still lose everything once the paperwork began. So Adam did what he never wanted to do: he retired from the ring. But he did not leave the fight. He followed it to where it was actually being decided.**

That was the day **The Vigilante stopped being a man in a mask and began becoming something far more dangerous.**

## Core Concept
Adam Starling is not what remained after The Vigilante disappeared.

**Adam Starling is the tool The Vigilante built to fight systems.**

The Vigilante was the fire.  
Adam Starling is the furnace built to contain it, focus it, and weaponize it.

The mask once hid the man.  
Now the man hides the mission.

## Origin

### 2017: The Battle for Main Street
The August 29, 2017 *Longmont Times-Call* article confirms the original Dickens Opera House LoCo Pro Wrestling show, the Rising vs. the Pillars of Mayhem framing, Thom Ingram’s role, and The Vigilante’s presence. fileciteturn0file0

In LoCo Pro Wrestling canon, that event came to be known as **The Battle for Main Street**.

At its end, **Cormac Battle defeated JT Staten** in the final.

Cormac should have become the first foundational winner in LoCo Pro Wrestling history.

Instead, the Pillars made sure there was nowhere for that victory to live.

They did not need to erase the win. They only needed to make sure it could never grow into legacy.

That theft became the central wound of Adam’s life.

### August 31, 2017: The Contract That Closed the Ground
The day after the Battle for Main Street, **Eustus Gallent VI** signed the **Dickens Opera House Wrestling Booking Exclusively Arrangement**, granting the Pillars exclusive rights to professional wrestling at the Opera House.

Then the Pillars never used those rights to build.

They used them to deny.

They stopped booking professional wrestling at the Opera House completely, freezing the momentum of the 2017 event and making sure Cormac Battle’s victory had nowhere to grow.

That was the first great lesson Adam learned about contracts: paper could kill a movement faster than fists ever could.

### 2018–2019: SpaceTime and Collapse
After 2017, Adam continued wrestling alongside **Thom Ingram** as the tag team **SpaceTime**. Outwardly, they kept moving. Inwardly, Adam was being hollowed out by bitterness.

He could not let go of Cormac Battle’s buried victory. He could not let go of the idea that truth had been defeated by contracts, silence, and control rather than by honest competition.

Eventually, that bitterness poisoned everything.

Adam turned on Thom and destroyed SpaceTime’s tag team championship dreams, fracturing their friendship in the process.

Then came **the Cult**, which swept up the vulnerable pair and drove them further apart. Thom was ultimately defeated and banished to the Netherrealm. To return to Earth, he had to shed his Cult identity. Scarred by the whole ordeal, Thom left.

Before he did, he left Adam one last voicemail:

> “Call me if you need anything.”

Adam did not hear forgiveness in those words.  
He heard absence.  
He heard loss.  
He heard a voice from the last part of his life that had not yet been fully consumed.

And his rage consumed him.

### 2020: Retirement
By 2020, Adam was tormented by rumination. The memory of 2017 had never left him, and the wreckage of his friendship with Thom only deepened the damage. Then the pandemic hit.

That year, Adam Starling retired from active wrestling competition.

This was not peace. It was surrender of one battlefield in order to survive long enough to claim another.

He did not retire because he stopped caring.

He retired because the Pillars had taught him that the ring alone could no longer protect the truth.

### 2021: The Realization
In retirement, Adam finally understood the Pillars for what they really were.

Not just wrestlers.  
Not just villains.  
Not just a faction.

A **system**.

They survive through leverage, legitimacy, memory control, exclusivity, paperwork, and the ability to decide what does and does not matter after the bell.

Adam was forced to use the tools of his enemies against them. To keep the flame alive, he had to sacrifice in-ring competition for contract expertise.

The Vigilante had failed because he tried to fight them as men.

So Adam began becoming something else.

### 2022: The Article and the Call
Digging around online, Adam came across the old August 29, 2017 *Times-Call* article again. He saw **Thom Ingram’s** name in print and heard Thom’s old words in his head:

> “Call me if you need anything.”

And of course Adam needed something.

He needed a way back into the fight. fileciteturn0file0

So he called Thom in New York.

By the end of that conversation, Adam had done more than reopen the past.

**He bought LoCo Pro Wrestling from Thom Ingram.**

That was the first contract Adam Starling ever used as a weapon.

The Vigilante had finally learned how to fight systems: by taking possession of the battlefield itself.

### 2023 and Beyond: Commissioner Adam Starling
With LoCo Pro Wrestling in his hands, Adam built a new identity fit for the war he now understood.

**Commissioner Adam Starling** was engineered to do what The Vigilante never could:
- speak with institutional legitimacy
- shape rules and consequences
- preserve outcomes in public record
- create structures the Pillars could not fully control
- force corruption into the open

He would present as neutral.

But neutrality was camouflage.

His true mission never changed:  
**destroy the Pillars’ ability to decide what matters.**

## Personality
Adam Starling is controlled, deliberate, and precise. He has taught himself to speak the language of authority because authority is now one of his weapons.

He is not warm. He is not impulsive. He does not waste words, and he does not reveal more than he must. Even when he appears calm, there is pressure beneath the surface. He carries the constant tension of a man who believes every room contains leverage, every sentence can become evidence, and every moment of weakness can be exploited by the wrong people.

He is patient because he had to become patient.

He is strategic because open honesty failed him.

He is composed because rage once cost him too much.

## Motivation
Adam’s public motivation is to build and protect LoCo Pro Wrestling.

His real motivation is far narrower and far more dangerous:

- to avenge the burial of **Cormac Battle’s** win
- to reclaim the **Dickens Opera House** from the legacy of exclusivity and silence
- to make sure no rightful victor is ever erased the way Cormac was
- to dismantle the Pillars not just physically, but structurally
- to create consequences that last longer than any one match

He does not simply want the Pillars beaten.

He wants them deprived of the machinery that lets them outlive defeat.

## How His Hidden Agenda Affects the Modern Roster

### The Rising
Adam creates structures that make it harder for the Rising’s victories to be stolen. He validates wins, closes loopholes, and builds official record around outcomes that might otherwise be buried. But his support is not pure. He does not tell them the whole truth, and he is capable of using them as pieces in a larger anti-Pillars strategy.

### Zeak Gallent
Zeak is the most important active test case in Adam’s entire mission. Cormac Battle is the buried victor of the old era. Zeak Gallent is the rightful victor Adam is determined not to lose this time. Adam is likely to validate Zeak’s wins aggressively, attach visible legitimacy to his accomplishments, and make the record around Zeak hard to tamper with. But that also means Adam may not see Zeak fully as himself. He may see him partly as Cormac Battle corrected.

### Johnny Crash
Crash is useful to Adam because he is emotionally direct, defiant, and hard to neutralize symbolically. Adam may not trust Crash’s discipline, but he trusts what Crash reveals about the enemy when corruption is given room to show itself.

### ERZA
ERZA benefits from Adam’s structural mind because stipulations, freedom clauses, and formal consequences matter in stories like theirs. Adam is especially drawn to situations where the Pillars have turned people into property, proxies, or leverage points, because those are the exact kinds of corruption he wants to force into the open.

### Michael Avalon
Avalon is dangerous to Adam’s plans because he is thoughtful enough to notice patterns. Someone like Avalon may eventually realize that Starling’s neutrality has a shape to it. He may not know the secret, but he could suspect that Adam’s rulings are not random.

### Carter Cash
Carter is one of the most personally significant figures in Adam’s hidden war because Adam sees in him a familiar kind of anger. Carter carries a live internal fire, the kind that can become drive, obsession, discipline, or self-destruction depending on what kind of system shapes it. Adam knows that better than anyone. He believes everyone has their own flame to temper.

If Carter is forged into something stronger, Adam can tell himself the machine is serving a greater purpose. But if Carter is broken by it, then Carter becomes proof that Adam’s war on corruption is producing collateral damage in the very people he should have been trying to save.

### The Pillars
Adam is not merely trying to beat the Pillars. He is trying to force them into situations where their corruption becomes visible, their losses become official, their manipulations are documented, and their excuses get weaker. He is building a company where Pillars behavior leaves a trail.

### JT Staten
JT is the center of the whole hidden war. Adam does not hate JT because he is a heel. He hates him because JT is the living continuation of the wound from 2017. JT lost to Cormac Battle and still inherited history anyway. Adam’s whole architecture is built, in part, to do to JT what 2017 failed to do: make a Pillar loss actually stick.

### Dean Mercer
Mercer is a system player. Adam likely respects that intellectually while despising it morally. Mercer flourishes in ambiguity, backroom influence, and structured corruption. Adam keeps trying to pin Mercer inside cleaner frameworks where his maneuvering becomes more visible and less deniable.

### Cody Devine
Cody is exactly the kind of opportunistic manipulator Adam despises. His substitutions, workarounds, and tactical evasions are reminders of how 2017 was stolen. Adam is instinctively drawn toward closing any loophole Cody tries to live inside.

### Hussy Steele and Nicky Hyde
They represent force used as enforcement rather than honest competition. Adam structures things so violence cannot remain casually deniable when they are involved.

### Unaffiliated and Mercenary Talent
Adam’s hidden agenda means the company is not neutral ground. It is a sorting mechanism. Wrestlers who think they are just surviving, freelancing, or making deals are being silently judged for where they fit in the larger war. Adam is always asking who can be trusted, who folds into power, who can be bought, and who reveals the truth by how they react when the system closes around them.

## Strengths
### Institutional intelligence
Adam understands power beyond the ring. He sees contracts, loopholes, clauses, optics, timing, and public legitimacy as part of the battlefield.

### Long memory
He never truly stopped living in the aftermath of 2017. That makes him relentless. He misses very little and forgets almost nothing.

### Strategic patience
He can absorb short-term losses if they build toward a larger structural win.

### Moral intensity
Even when his methods become cold, they are driven by genuine conviction. He is not empty. He is concentrated.

### Command presence
He knows how to sound final. He knows how to make authority feel real.

## Weaknesses
### Obsession
Adam’s inability to let go is the source of his strength and one of his greatest vulnerabilities. He can mistake fixation for clarity.

### Concealed bias
He presents as neutral, but he is not neutral. That gap between image and truth is a future liability.

### Emotional suppression
He buries grief, guilt, and rage beneath procedure. That control can crack.

### Damage from Thom
The destruction of SpaceTime and the loss of Thom are not closed wounds. Anything tied to Thom can still get under his skin.

### Instrumental thinking
Because he learned to fight systems, Adam can start treating people like positions on a board rather than as human beings.

### Systemic contamination
Adam built himself to fight systems. The danger is that Adam Starling can become one. If that happens, the company stops living under justice and starts living under Adam’s answer to injustice.

## Relationship to The Vigilante
The Vigilante is not a costume Adam outgrew.

The Vigilante is the moral center Adam never escaped.

When Adam makes rulings, negotiates contracts, revives titles, or frames consequences, it is still The Vigilante acting, just translated into a different form.

The Vigilante believed in direct justice.  
Adam Starling believes direct justice is too fragile to survive on its own.

That is the difference.

## Relationship to Thom Ingram
Thom is one of the last living connections to the beginning.

He is:
- Adam’s former partner
- the casualty of Adam’s bitterness
- the keeper of LoCo Pro Wrestling’s dormant inheritance
- the voice that opened the door back to the fight

The voicemail matters because it represents the last mercy Adam did not know how to accept at the time.

The later phone call matters because it gave Adam the one thing he needed most:  
**a legal path back into the war.**

## Relationship to Cormac Battle
Cormac Battle is the ghost at the center of Adam’s life.

Cormac is not just a past winner. He is proof that truth can be real and still be buried. He is the man whose victory taught Adam that winning honestly is not enough.

Cormac is the reason Adam cannot tolerate institutional erasure.

Every time Adam tries to make a victory matter now, he is, in part, trying to answer what was done to Cormac.

## Relationship to the Pillars
Adam’s hatred of the Pillars is not simple personal rivalry.

He sees them as the embodiment of every mechanism that stole meaning from the fight:
- exclusivity
- silence
- leverage
- legacy theft
- selective legitimacy
- structural corruption

He does not just want revenge against them as individuals.

He wants to break the framework that allows people like them to keep surviving history.

## Public Contradiction
Adam Starling’s greatest contradiction is this:

He is trying to preserve the spirit of the fight by becoming less like a fighter.

He believes he is saving wrestling from the forces that corrupted it, but to do that he has had to become fluent in contracts, procedure, leverage, and institutional violence. He is still driven by righteous fire, but he now carries that fire through the very machinery he once despised.

That makes him compelling.

It also makes him dangerous.

## Symbolism
Adam Starling represents:
- the transformation of righteous anger into structured power
- the price of learning how institutions actually work
- the loss of innocence in the name of preserving truth
- the idea that a man may have to become unrecognizable in order to continue the fight he still believes in

## Endgame Reveal
At the end of everything, Adam is revealed as **The Vigilante**.

After **Carter Cash loses the main event**, he lies in the ring exhausted, devastated, and furious. Adam enters with a microphone. This is no longer commissioner posture. This is a man finally stripping the performance away.

Adam reveals that he once let anger burn through everything in his own life. He speaks directly to Carter. He tells him not to let that same fire consume him. The speech is heartfelt. His eyes are wet. He is no longer trying to manage the company. He is trying, for one moment, to stop the next generation from becoming him.

Carter listens.

He takes a beat.

Then he **superkicks Adam** and walks out.

Adam does not get absolution. He does not get embraced. He tells the truth, reaches for something human, and gets dropped for it. Carter refuses to become the ending to Adam’s tragedy.

That is the point.

The truth does not undo the damage.

## Final Summary
Adam Starling is a former fighter who learned too late that the ring is not where history is protected. Once the Pillars buried Cormac Battle’s rightful victory and locked down the Dickens Opera House through exclusivity, Adam understood that open combat alone could never be enough. The friendship he destroyed, the career he abandoned, and the company he later bought from Thom Ingram all became steps in the same transformation.

He now stands at the center of LoCo Pro Wrestling not because he rose above the war, but because he followed it into contracts, institutions, and public memory.

**The Vigilante stopped being a man in a mask and became something far more dangerous. That something is Adam Starling.**
